— Process

How I approach problems.

01

Start with the system

Before solutions, I map the system: what's the input, what's the expected output, where does it break, and who's downstream. The problem is almost never where it appears.

02

Find the real constraint

Most problems have a root cause that's one or two levels below the stated one. I'd rather spend time finding that than building something elegant on top of a wrong assumption.

03

Build something real, fast

I don't pitch ideas without a prototype. Even a rough version reveals tradeoffs you can't see on a whiteboard. I move fast to the point where feedback is possible.

04

Measure and decide

The output is only as good as what you do with it. I track what matters — not vanity metrics — and use it to decide whether to push, pivot, or stop.

— About

Technical depth. Product judgment.

I'm a CS grad finishing a Master's in Engineering Management at Johns Hopkins. My background is technical. The way I work is product-oriented. That combination is deliberate — I wanted to understand systems deeply enough to know what's possible, and business context well enough to know what's worth doing.

At Weber Shandwick, I was the first hire into a newly created AI analytics role. No template, no senior person to shadow in the same function. Most of what I built, I built by figuring out what the team actually needed — not what AI could theoretically do. That distinction matters to me.

I care about the difference between AI that impresses and AI that gets used. Building the second kind requires understanding the workflow, the user, and the failure modes — not just the model.

The hard part isn't having an LLM. It's designing the workflow around it so the output is reliable enough to trust.
